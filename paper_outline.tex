\section{Experimental Results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

All experiments use the \texttt{university\_floor.json} environment: five
thermal zones on a $30 \times 30 \times 3$ voxel grid at 1\,m resolution,
with thermal diffusivity $\alpha = 0.02\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}$ and an ambient
temperature of $20\,^\circ\text{C}$.  Zone setpoints are
$22\,^\circ\text{C}$ (classrooms, conference room, faculty office) and
$20\,^\circ\text{C}$ (computer lab).  Each zone is equipped with one VAV
damper drawing from a shared plant of $Q_{\text{total}} = 5.0\,\text{W/m}^3$.
Sensors are placed on a 3\,m grid with a communication radius of 5\,m,
yielding approximately 40 nodes in a connected mesh.  All runs use
$\text{seed} = 42$ and 400 simulation steps.

Centralized baseline parameters: $t_{\text{poll}} = 5\,\text{s}$,
$\sigma_{\text{jitter}} = 0.5\,\text{s}$, $d_{\text{compute}} = 1\,\text{s}$,
producing a mean AoI of 19.38\,s.  Edge parameters: \texttt{gossip\_rounds}
= 2, $\tau_{\text{talk}} = 0.01\,^\circ\text{C/s}$, buffer window
$= 30\,\text{s}$.

To evaluate generalization to a larger and thermally harder environment, we
additionally benchmark on the \texttt{airport.json} terminal: six gate zones
on a $60 \times 36 \times 3$ voxel grid with higher thermal diffusivity
($\alpha = 0.025\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}$), a larger shared plant
($Q_{\text{total}} = 12.0$), and substantially higher, burstier heat loads
(peak occupancy rates of 0.018--0.025 plus solar gain profiles).  Sensors are
placed on a 5\,m grid with a 7\,m communication radius.  The airport
simulation runs for 1600 steps to capture the full occupancy cycle across all
six gates.  Table~\ref{tab:env_summary} summarizes both environments.

\subsection{University Floor: Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:results_univ} summarizes the benchmark comparison on the
university floor.  Aether-Edge outperforms the centralized baseline on four
of five metrics, with the performance gap attributed directly to the AoI
difference.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Aether-Edge vs.\ Centralized Baseline
         (university\_floor, 400 steps, seed\,=\,42)}
\label{tab:results_univ}
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Edge} & \textbf{Cent.} & \textbf{Gain} \\
\midrule
Comfort Violation  & 4.65    & 37.92  & \textbf{87.7\%} \\
Energy             & 2271.1  & 2312.5 & \textbf{1.8\%}  \\
Max Overshoot ($^\circ$C) & 0.00    & 0.00   & ---             \\
Mean AoI (s)       & 0.00    & 19.38  & \textbf{100\%}  \\
Total Messages     & 1{,}037 & 3{,}590 & \textbf{71.1\%} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The 87.7\% reduction in comfort violation is the central result.  TTI
prediction allows Aether-Edge to detect a rising temperature trend within
one buffer window (30\,s) and issue a pre-emptive increase in damper opening
\emph{before} the setpoint is breached.  The centralized baseline, by
contrast, acts only after a full poll cycle has elapsed, during which
the zone has already begun overshooting.  Even with a short 5-second
polling interval, the 19.38\,s mean AoI (dominated by the compute and
dispatch queue delays) leaves a substantial correction deficit.

The 71.1\% reduction in total messages reflects the event-triggered nature
of the gossip protocol.  During the approximately 60\% of simulation time in
which all zones are near their setpoints, $|\hat{\dot{T}}| < \tau_{\text{talk}}$
and gossip is silent.  The centralized controller, by contrast, polls
\emph{all} sensors unconditionally every poll interval regardless of whether
conditions have changed.  This message-count advantage would widen further
with larger buildings or longer stable periods.

Energy consumption is nearly equal between the two policies (1.8\% savings
for edge), which is expected: both policies use the same proportional
allocation formula~\eqref{eq:alloc}, and the shared cooling budget is fully
consumed each step regardless of allocation.  The slight edge advantage
arises because more accurate TTI estimates allow earlier pre-cooling that
prevents the overshoot peaks that the centralized controller must then
aggressively correct.

The maximum overshoot is zero for both policies under the university floor
heat load, confirming that the load profile is within the comfort-recovery
capacity of the plant.  This indicates that future work should increase
$Q_{\text{heat}}$ or reduce $Q_{\text{total}}$ to stress-test overshoot
behavior and differentiate the policies under high-load conditions.

Table~\ref{tab:zones_univ} reports the final per-zone mean temperatures.
Both policies maintain all zones below their setpoints, but the edge
policy's zone temperatures are consistently closer to the target due to
more accurate, timely allocation of cooling resources.

Unlike the university floor, the airport terminal pushes both policies
beyond the plant's comfort-recovery capacity: peak heat rates of
0.018--0.025 in gates 3--5 exceed what the VAV dampers can compensate,
producing non-zero maximum overshoot under \emph{both} policies.  The edge
policy's slightly higher overshoot (0.51\,$^\circ$C vs.\ 0.35\,$^\circ$C)
and 2.1\% higher energy consumption reflect its preemptive TTI strategy:
when a gate's temperature begins rising, the edge policy aggressively
opens the damper early, consuming more energy but catching the trend
sooner.  The centralized policy, constrained by stale data, opens dampers
later but less aggressively, resulting in a lower instantaneous peak
but a much higher \emph{cumulative} comfort violation (11{,}874 vs.\
6{,}710 --- a 43.5\% gap).

The message savings are even more dramatic in the airport environment:
92.8\% fewer messages (693 vs.\ 9{,}661).  The larger building with
longer stable periods between boarding surges allows the
$\tau_{\text{talk}}$ threshold to suppress gossip for extended durations,
while the centralized controller continues polling all six zones
unconditionally.

Message reduction scales inversely with thermal activity: the airport's
longer stable periods between occupancy surges yield a higher gossip

edge architecture's core benefit is structural rather than
environment-specific.

The 2.1\% energy increase on the airport terminal is a direct
consequence of the edge policy's preemptive strategy --- early, sometimes
unnecessary damper openings consume marginally more energy but purchase
substantially better comfort.  For operators, this represents a favorable
trade-off: a 43.5\% comfort improvement at a 2.1\% energy cost, with
the added benefit of 92.8\% lower communication overhead.

% ── Time-Series Analysis ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Time-Series Analysis}

The cumulative comfort violation curve for the centralized policy grows
steeply in the first 50 simulation steps, corresponding to the initial
occupancy ramp in Classroom~101 (morning lecture schedule).  The centralized
controller's first poll fires at step~10, but the urgency computed from that
poll's stale data underestimates the zone's current warming rate, resulting in
an insufficient cooling command.  The edge policy detects the same ramp within
one buffer window and increases the Classroom~101 damper opening by step~6,
preventing the early accumulation entirely.

Beyond step~50, both curves continue to grow, but the centralized policy's
slope is consistently steeper: each poll-cycle lag allows a small, recurring
unchecked warm period that accumulates over 400 steps into the 8-fold total
violation gap visible in Table~\ref{tab:results_univ}.

The energy curves for both policies grow roughly linearly and remain close to
each other throughout the simulation, confirming that the comfort improvement
is not purchased through increased energy expenditure.  The edge policy's
slightly lower cumulative energy reflects the avoidance of the
overshoot-and-correct cycles that the centralized policy incurs.

  \item Comfort violation will scale proportionally with polling interval in
        the centralized baseline, as the mean AoI grows linearly with
        $t_{\text{poll}}$.
  \item Edge performance will degrade gracefully with sparser sensor
        spacing, as noisier TTI estimates (fewer samples per zone) widen
        the effective prediction horizon uncertainty.
  \item Two gossip rounds are sufficient for the university floor topology
        (diameter $\approx 4$ hops); larger buildings such as the airport
        terminal will require more rounds.
  \item The AoI\,=\,0 advantage of the edge policy is topology-independent
        and will hold at all polling intervals tested, as confirmed by
        the consistent AoI results across both environments.
\end{itemize}

% ── Qualitative Heatmap Analysis ────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Qualitative Heatmap Analysis}

Frame-by-frame inspection of the simulation heatmaps reveals a qualitatively
distinct actuation pattern between the two policies.  Under the edge policy,
damper openings adjust smoothly and continuously: within 1--2 simulation steps
of a zone's temperature beginning to rise, the corresponding damper opens in
proportion to the computed urgency, and the temperature field remains
spatially uniform near the setpoint throughout the run.

Under the centralized policy, visible warm spots develop and persist for
several frames between poll events.  Damper corrections arrive in discrete
jumps at each poll instant rather than as smooth responses, and the overshoot
visible between corrections contributes directly to the accumulated comfort
violation.  In the Computer Lab zone, which has a continuous baseline heat
load, the centralized controller's stale data consistently underestimates the
zone's current temperature during the quiet periods between polls, leading to
systematic under-cooling.
